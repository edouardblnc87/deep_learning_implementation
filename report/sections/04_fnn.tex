\section{Feedforward Neural Network}
\label{sec:fnn}

% ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Model and Validation}

\subsubsection*{Architecture}

The baseline FNN maps the $L$ most recent signed log-returns to a scalar
one-day-ahead volatility forecast through two hidden layers:
\[
  \mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^{L}
  \;\xrightarrow{\;\text{Dense}_{32}\;}\;
  \mathbb{R}^{32}
  \;\xrightarrow{\;\text{Dense}_{16}\;}\;
  \mathbb{R}^{16}
  \;\xrightarrow{\;\text{Dense}_{1}\;}\;
  \hat{y} \in \mathbb{R}_{+}.
\]
Both hidden layers use \textbf{ReLU} activations and \textbf{He normal}
initialisation; the output neuron is linear. This choice of activation directly
addresses the \emph{symmetry blindness} of the linear baseline: two composed ReLU
layers can approximate $\lvert x\rvert$ from signed inputs, recovering the
signal in $r_t$ without manual feature engineering.

\subsubsection*{Implementation validation}

The custom NumPy framework (\texttt{dl\_utils}) is cross-checked against an
equivalent PyTorch implementation before any tuning.

\begin{table}[H]
  \centering
  \caption{Custom NumPy implementation vs.\ PyTorch reference and linear baseline (lag~$=10$).}
  \label{tab:fnn_validation}
  \begin{tabular}{lcc}
    \toprule
    Implementation & MAE & Note \\
    \midrule
    Custom \texttt{dl\_utils} (NumPy) & 0.6046 & dropout $= 0.5$ on layer~1 \\
    PyTorch reference                 & 0.5850 & no dropout \\
    Linear regression baseline        & 0.6377 & \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

Both neural implementations beat the linear baseline.
The small gap between them stems from the dropout regularisation applied only in
the NumPy model: dropout slows convergence, raising the early-stopped MAE slightly.

% ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Hyperparameter Tuning}

All experiments in this section share the same default training protocol unless
stated otherwise: MSE loss, batch size~32, maximum 300~epochs, early stopping
with patience~$=10$ evaluated every 5~epochs.

% ──────────────────────────────────────
\subsubsection{Activation Function}

Four activations are compared on the $32{\to}16{\to}1$ architecture with plain
SGD ($\alpha=10^{-3}$, learning rate).

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/figure_41.png}
  \caption{Validation loss (MSE) over 300 epochs for four activations.
    ReLU (red, MAE~$=0.607$) reaches the best test MAE; Sigmoid (orange) is still
    declining at epoch~300.}
  \label{fig:fnn_activations}
\end{figure}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Sigmoid} (MAE~$0.612$): gradient saturation dramatically slows
        learning; the validation loss has not plateaued by epoch~300. The
        model does ultimately converge at this shallow depth after
        \texttt{StandardScaler} normalisation, but requires far more epochs
        than the others.
  \item \textbf{Tanh} (MAE~$0.613$): converges the fastest (before epoch~30),
        yet it reaches the \emph{worst} test MAE. Fast training convergence does
        not imply good generalisation.
  \item \textbf{ReLU} (MAE~$0.607$, best): rapid, stable convergence with mild
        oscillations attributable to occasional dead-neuron events.
  \item \textbf{LeakyReLU} (MAE~$0.608$): virtually identical to ReLU.
        After \texttt{StandardScaler} normalisation the input distribution is
        centred, so the dying-neuron advantage of LeakyReLU is negligible.
\end{itemize}

\noindent\textbf{Decision:} ReLU for all subsequent experiments.

% ──────────────────────────────────────
\subsubsection{Weight Initialisation}

Five initialisation schemes are compared, all paired with ReLU.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/figure_42.png}
  \caption{Convergence by initialisation scheme. Standard $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$
    (red) triggers a gradient explosion at epoch~$\approx 75$ (MSE peaks at~3.3)
    but ultimately converges to the lowest test MAE. He normal/uniform converge
    stably and reach comparable performance.}
  \label{fig:fnn_init}
\end{figure}

\begin{table}[H]
  \centering
  \caption{Final test MAE by initialisation scheme (ReLU, SGD $\alpha=10^{-3}$).}
  \label{tab:fnn_init}
  \begin{tabular}{lc}
    \toprule
    Initialisation & MAE \\
    \midrule
    Standard $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$ & 0.604 \\
    He normal                   & 0.607 \\
    He uniform                  & 0.607 \\
    Glorot normal               & 0.613 \\
    LeCun normal                & 0.616 \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

Standard initialisation achieves the \emph{lowest} final MAE but only through an
unreliable mechanism: the gradient explosion near epoch~75 (MSE~$\to 3.3$)
accidentally perturbs the weights out of a shallow basin. On a harder
problem or a deeper network this spike would cause permanent divergence.
He normal reaches near-identical performance ($+0.003$) with \emph{monotone,
predictable} convergence, and is theoretically motivated: for ReLU layers,
$\operatorname{Var}(W) = 2/n_{\mathrm{in}}$ (where $n_{\mathrm{in}}$ is the number
of inputs to the layer) ensures that activation variance is preserved layer-to-layer, preventing both vanishing and exploding signals.

\noindent\textbf{Decision:} He normal for all subsequent experiments.

% ──────────────────────────────────────
\subsubsection{Optimizer and Momentum}

\paragraph{Learning rate and effect of momentum.}

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/figure_43.png}
  \caption{Convergence by optimizer. Plain SGD at three learning rates and
    SGD with momentum $\mu=0.9$ (with and without LR decay). Momentum
    dominates all plain-SGD variants; adding decay changes nothing under
    early stopping.}
  \label{fig:fnn_optimizer}
\end{figure}

\begin{itemize}
  \item $\alpha=10^{-4}$: convergence is too slow (MAE~$0.629$ at epoch~300,
        still declining).
  \item $\alpha=10^{-3}$: stable convergence (MAE~$0.607$).
  \item $\alpha=10^{-2}$: early overshoot followed by convergence to a worse
        minimum (MAE~$0.618$).
  \item SGD + momentum $\mu=0.9$ (MAE~$0.591$): reaches the final MAE of
        plain SGD ($\alpha=10^{-3}$) roughly 10$\times$ faster, then converges
        further.
  \item SGD + momentum + LR decay (MAE~$0.591$): identical to plain momentum.
        Early stopping triggers before the decay schedule has a meaningful
        effect, rendering it redundant.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Momentum sweep.}

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/figure_44.png}
  \caption{Test MAE as a function of momentum coefficient~$\mu$.
    Performance improves steadily to $\mu=0.95$; at $\mu=0.99$ the model
    diverges.}
  \label{fig:fnn_momentum}
\end{figure}

\begin{table}[H]
  \centering
  \caption{MAE by momentum coefficient (He normal, ReLU, lag~$=10$).}
  \label{tab:fnn_momentum}
  \begin{tabular}{lc}
    \toprule
    $\mu$ & MAE \\
    \midrule
    0.00 & 0.607 \\
    0.50 & 0.602 \\
    0.70 & 0.602 \\
    0.90 & 0.591 \\
    0.95 & \textbf{0.587} \\
    0.99 & diverges \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

Performance improves steadily up to $\mu=0.95$, then diverges at $\mu=0.99$.
At very high momentum the accumulated velocity overpowers the corrective gradient
signal, causing the weights to oscillate with growing amplitude.

\noindent\textbf{Decision:} $\mu=0.95$ for all subsequent experiments.

\paragraph{Loss surface geometry.}

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/figure_45.png}
  \caption{2-D loss-surface contours (top) and 3-D surfaces (bottom) with
    weight trajectories for plain SGD (left, blue) and SGD+momentum $\mu=0.9$
    (right, green). Momentum follows a wider arc but descends to a darker
    (lower-loss) region.}
  \label{fig:fnn_surface}
\end{figure}

Plain SGD takes short, monotone gradient steps and stalls in the first basin it
enters. Momentum accumulates velocity in the consistent gradient direction,
allowing it to traverse flat saddle regions and settle in a deeper minimum ---
visible in the contour plot as the green trajectory reaching a darker zone
unreachable by the blue one.

\subsubsection{Temporal Window}

The lag $L$ is swept across $\{1,3,5,10,20,30,45,60\}$ using the best
configuration found above (ReLU, He normal, SGD $\mu=0.95$).

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/figure_49.png}
  \caption{Test MAE as a function of lag window~$L$. Performance peaks at
    $L=3$ (MAE~$0.578$, dashed orange); it generally increases for larger
    windows with a local dip at lag~$45$.}
  \label{fig:fnn_lag}
\end{figure}

\begin{table}[H]
  \centering
  \caption{Test MAE by lag window (ReLU, He normal, SGD $\mu=0.95$).}
  \label{tab:fnn_lag}
  \begin{tabular}{lc}
    \toprule
    Lag $L$ & MAE \\
    \midrule
    1  & 0.629 \\
    \textbf{3}  & \textbf{0.578} \\
    5  & 0.589 \\
    10 & 0.591 \\
    20 & 0.602 \\
    30 & 0.608 \\
    45 & 0.600 \\
    60 & 0.614 \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

Performance peaks at $L=3$ and generally degrades beyond, though not strictly
monotonically (lag~$=45$ recovers slightly below lag~$=30$, a seed-dependent
fluctuation). The pattern reflects a structural limitation of the FNN: all lag
features receive independently learned fixed weights, so the network has no
mechanism to discount distant, noisy lags. Beyond 3~days, the additional
information added by older returns is outweighed by the noise they introduce.

\noindent\textbf{Decision:} $L=3$ for all subsequent experiments.

% ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Diagnostics: Inside the Trained Network}

The figures in this section do not drive hyperparameter choices; they explain
\emph{why} the chosen configuration (ReLU + He normal) works and why the bad
configuration (Sigmoid + standard init) fails.

% ──────────────────────────────────────
\subsubsection{Weight and Activation Distributions}

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/figure_46.png}
  \caption{\emph{Good} configuration (ReLU + He normal). Rows: weight
    distributions, pre-activations, post-activations. Columns: layers~1, 2,
    output. Blue~$=$ before training; red~$=$ after.
    All three layers show substantial weight spreading and healthy activation
    ranges.}
  \label{fig:fnn_good}
\end{figure}

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/figure_47.png}
  \caption{\emph{Bad} configuration (Sigmoid + standard init). Same layout.
    Weights barely move during training; layer~2 post-activations collapse to
    the narrow interval $[0.9,\,1.0]$ --- the saturation zone --- transmitting
    essentially no information to the output.}
  \label{fig:fnn_bad}
\end{figure}

With ReLU + He, weight distributions broaden during training and pre-activations
span a wide range: every neuron is active and contributing.
With Sigmoid + standard init, the gradient explosion compresses post-activations
into Sigmoid's saturation zone; backpropagated gradients become near-zero and
learning stalls. The distribution collapse renders vanishing gradients directly
visible without gradient inspection.

% ──────────────────────────────────────
\subsubsection{Gradient Flow}

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/figure_48.png}
  \caption{Gradient $\ell_2$-norms per layer over 150~epochs.
    Left (ReLU + He): all layers maintain comparable norms throughout.
    Right (Sigmoid + standard): early layers receive gradients 2--3$\times$
    smaller than the output layer.}
  \label{fig:fnn_grad}
\end{figure}

With ReLU, the derivative equals~1 for every active neuron, so gradients
propagate backward without attenuation: all layers update at the same scale.
With Sigmoid, the derivative is bounded by~$0.25$ and approaches~$0$ in
saturation; compounded across layers this concentrates learning at the output
while the input layer stagnates. In a deeper network this effect would be
catastrophic.

% ──────────────────────────────────────
\subsubsection{Weight Evolution During Training}

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/figure_412.png}
  \caption{Weight distribution snapshots at epochs 0, 10, 30, 100, 200
    (ReLU + He normal, best config). All three layers exhibit progressive
    spreading from a tight initialisation to broad, learned distributions.}
  \label{fig:fnn_evolution}
\end{figure}

At epoch~0 the He initialisation produces narrow Gaussian-like distributions in
all layers. By epoch~200, layers~1 and~2 have spread considerably and the output
layer has developed a multimodal structure. The monotone spreading across every
layer confirms that no part of the network is frozen or dead: gradient updates
reach and reshape all weights throughout training.

% ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Structural Limitations}

Despite a 9\,\% improvement over the linear baseline, the FNN has two
architectural limitations that no amount of hyperparameter tuning can address.

% ──────────────────────────────────────
\subsubsection{Order Blindness}

The FNN receives the $L$ lag features as a flat, unordered vector.
To measure how much temporal ordering it \emph{implicitly} exploits, we evaluate
the trained model on 30 random permutations of the input features and report the
mean MAE gap.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/figure_410.png}
  \caption{Test MAE for original feature order (blue) vs.\ randomly shuffled
    features (red, mean of 30~trials) at each lag window. The degradation gap
    grows from $+0.013$ at lag~$3$ to $+0.032$ at lag~$60$.}
  \label{fig:fnn_order}
\end{figure}

\begin{table}[H]
  \centering
  \caption{Original vs.\ shuffled-input MAE by lag window.}
  \label{tab:fnn_order}
  \begin{tabular}{lccc}
    \toprule
    Lag $L$ & Original & Shuffled & Gap \\
    \midrule
    3  & 0.578 & 0.591 & $+$0.013 \\
    5  & 0.589 & 0.608 & $+$0.019 \\
    10 & 0.591 & 0.599 & $+$0.008 \\
    20 & 0.602 & 0.626 & $+$0.024 \\
    45 & 0.600 & 0.626 & $+$0.026 \\
    60 & 0.614 & 0.646 & $+$0.032 \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

The network has learned to exploit recency through its weight magnitudes ---
recent lags receive larger learned weights --- but this is an emergent property
of optimisation, not a guarantee of the architecture. The growing gap with lag
window confirms that richer temporal structure is present and the FNN partially
uses it; a recurrent architecture would process inputs sequentially, making
temporal ordering a structural rather than a learned property.

% ──────────────────────────────────────
\subsubsection{Regime Blindness}

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/figure_411.png}
  \caption{Left: predicted vs.\ true volatility coloured by regime
    (calm~$=$~blue, crisis~$=$~red). Crisis predictions cluster near zero
    regardless of the true spike magnitude. Right: MAE by regime ---
    crisis MAE is $2.77\times$ higher than calm MAE.}
  \label{fig:fnn_regime}
\end{figure}

\begin{table}[H]
  \centering
  \caption{FNN MAE by volatility regime (75th-percentile threshold, $L=3$;
    note: the Data section uses a 90th-percentile definition).}
  \label{tab:fnn_regime}
  \begin{tabular}{lcc}
    \toprule
    Regime & MAE & Share \\
    \midrule
    Calm   ($\sigma_t < \sigma^{*}_{75}$)     & 0.402 & 75\,\% \\
    Crisis ($\sigma_t \geq \sigma^{*}_{75}$)  & 1.112 & 25\,\% \\
    \midrule
    Crisis / Calm ratio & $2.77\times$ & \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

The FNN makes each prediction from a static 3-day snapshot and then resets its
state entirely. It cannot accumulate the context of a sustained volatility
cluster building over days or weeks. Without persistent memory,
\emph{``high volatility today $\Rightarrow$ high volatility tomorrow''}
is not learnable.

% ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Summary}

\begin{table}[H]
  \centering
  \caption{Best FNN configuration and rationale.}
  \label{tab:fnn_best}
  \begin{tabular}{lll}
    \toprule
    Parameter & Choice & Rationale \\
    \midrule
    Activation    & ReLU              & Best test MAE; stable gradient propagation \\
    Initialisation & He normal        & Stable convergence; variance-preserving for ReLU \\
    Optimizer     & SGD $\mu=0.95$    & Fastest convergence; best generalisation \\
    Lag window    & $L=3$             & Beyond 3 days, distant lags add noise \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

With this configuration the FNN achieves \textbf{MAE~$=0.579$} on the test set
versus \textbf{0.638} for the linear baseline --- a \textbf{9\,\% improvement}
driven entirely by the non-linear activation resolving symmetry blindness.

Two structural limitations, demonstrated empirically, motivate the architectures
developed in the following sections:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Order blindness}: the FNN has no positional encoding; a
        recurrent architecture is needed to make temporal ordering a structural
        property rather than an optimisation artefact.
  \item \textbf{Regime blindness}: without a persistent hidden state, the model
        cannot exploit volatility clustering; an RNN is needed to carry
        context across time steps.
\end{itemize}
